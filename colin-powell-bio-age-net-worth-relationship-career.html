From 2001 to 2005, Colin Powell, a four-star general, politician, statesman, and diplomat, assumed the role of the 65th Secretary of State of the United States. Preceding him in stature was the inaugural African-American Secretary of State.

Powell, who was born in the South Bronx and earned his degree from the United States Military Academy in June 1958, assumed the rank of Army second lieutenant upon his commissioning. Subsequently, in 1989, he was elevated to the position of Commander of the United States Army Forces Command.

From 1988 until 1989, he served as the 12th Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff and the 16th National Security Advisor of the United States. (1989-93).

For his years of military service, he was honored with the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the Congressional Gold Medal, the Presidential Citizens Medal, and the Secretary’s Distinguished Service Award.

“My American Journey” (1995), an autobiography he had authored, and “It Worked for Me, Lessons in Life and Leadership” (2012) were both books he had published. Despite being entirely immunized, he passed away on October 18, 2021, at the age of 84, from complications associated with COVID-19.
